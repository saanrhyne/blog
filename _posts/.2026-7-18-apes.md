# How to Get a 5 on APES

APES is often considered an "easy" AP class, and the easiest of the AP sciences. While a lot of APES can be considered common sense or relatively simple to underestand, the course is still an AP class that goes fairly in-depth on some aspects of environmental science. APES can be a fun class when paired with labs, projects, and group work, or a boring class full of lectures.

Regardless of your interest or knowledge of environmental science, this guide can help you get an A in your class and a 5 on the APES exam.

*Note:* This article is meant to be a fairly comprehensive guide that, if followed properly, will substantially increase your preparedness for the Advanced Placement Environmental Science examination. However, I cannot guarantee any single result on your classwork and AP exam. ## Understanding the Course & Exam
### Units of Study
APES has 9 units, all of which are fairly long. This class is chock-full of content, but, luckily, there's a lot of overlap and areas to compound your knowledge.

Unit 3, in my opinion, is the easiest unit, while unit 4 is the most difficult. The biogeochemical cycles were also difficult to remember. Unit 9 makes up the most of the exam, but is largely a combination of previous units and topics, so don't stress about it too much!

Here is a breakdown of key concepts in each topic/set of topics for you to learn:

**Unit 1:** The Living World - Ecosystems *(6-8% of the Exam)*
* 1.1 - Introduction to Ecosystems
  * Predator-prey relationships
  * Symbiosis - mutualism (+/+), commensalism (+/0), parasitism (+/-)
  * Competition, resource partitioning
  * Memorize these relationships and terms!
* 1.2-1.3 - Terrestrial and Aquatic Biomes
  * Taiga, rainforests, seasonal forests, desert, tundra, etc
  * Streams, rivers, oceans, coral reefs, etc
  * Memorize key characteristics of these biomes
  * Know how to read climatographs
* 1.4-1.7 - Biogeochemical Cycles
  * Carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, water cycles
  * Understand the key reservoirs and movements
  * All are powered by the sun except phosphorus, which doesn't have an atmospheric phase
* 1.8-1.11 - Energy & Matter Cycling
  * Net primary productivity = Gross primary productivity - Respiration
  * Trophic levels - producer, primary consumer, secondary consumer, etc
  * 10% of energy is transfered from one trophic level to the next
  * Arrows show the direction of movement in food webs/chains

**Unit 2:** The Living World - Biodiversity *(6-8% of the Exam)*
* 2.1 - Introduction to Biodiversity
  * 3 types - genetic, species, habitat
  * More biodiversity = better response to stressors
  * Species richness vs evenness
  * Specialist vs generalist species
* 2.2 - Ecosystem Services
  * Provisoning, regulating, cultural, supporting services
  * Regulating and supporting are the hardest to differentiate
* 2.3 - Island Biogeography
  * Most island species are specialists
  * Larger and closer islands have more species
  * Smaller and farther islands have less species
* 2.4-2.6 - Adaptations in Ecosystems
  * Tolerance - range of conditions an organizsm can survive in
  * Natural disruptions to ecosystems (ie. wildfires)
  * Adaptations can occur in both the short-term and long-term
* 2.7 - Ecological Succession
  * Primary succession - start from bare rock
  * Secondary succession - start from some soil
  * Pioneer, keystone, indicator species

**Unit 3:** Populations *(10-15% of the Exam)*
* 3.1-3.2 - Types of Species
  * Generalist vs specialist species
  * K-selected vs r-selected species
* 3.3-3.5 - General Population Dynamics
  * Type I, II, and III survivorship curves
  * Carrying capacity, overshoot, dieback
  * Population growth is limited by resources
* 3.6-3.9 - Human Populations
  * Age structure diagrams
  * Total fertility rate - 2.1 is replacement level
  * Birth rates, IMR, death rates
  * Rule of 70
  * Demographic transition model

**Unit 4:** Earth Systems and Resources *(10-15% of the Exam)*
* 4.1 - Plate Tectonics
  * Convergent, divergent, transform boundaries
  * San Andreas Fault
* 4.2-4.3 - Soil
  * Soil formation - weathering, deposition
  * OAEBCR soil horizons
  * Sand, silt, and clay
  * Permeability, porosity, fertility
  * Reading soil texture traingles
* 4.4-4.5 - Atmosphere & Wind
  * 78% Nitrogen, 21% Oxygen, 0.9% Argon
  * Troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, thermosphere, exosphere
  * Westerlies & eastern trade winds
  * Coriolis effect
* 4.6 - Watersheds
  * All of the land that drains into a body of water
  * Area, length, slope, soil, vegetation types
* 4.7-4.8 - Seasons & Climate
  * Angle of insolation, albedo
  * Solar radiation at the equator vs poles
  * Ocean's influence on weather and climate
  * Rain shadow effect
* 4.9 - El Nino and La Nina
  * Remember the difference!
  * Peruvian upwelling

**Unit 5:** Land and Water Use *(10-15% of the Exam)*
**Unit 6:** Energy Resources and Consumption *(10-15% of the Exam)*
**Unit 7:** Atmospheric Pollution *(7-10% of the Exam)*
**Unit 8:** Aquatic and Terrestrial Pollution *(7-10% of the Exam)*
**Unit 9:** Global Change *(15-20% of the Exam)*

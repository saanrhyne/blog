# How to Get a 5 on APES

APES is often considered an "easy" AP class, and the easiest of the AP sciences. While a lot of APES can be considered common sense or relatively simple to underestand, the course is still an AP class that goes fairly in-depth on some aspects of environmental science. APES can be a fun class when paired with labs, projects, and group work, or a boring class full of lectures.

Regardless of your interest or knowledge of environmental science, this guide can help you get an A in your class and a 5 on the APES exam.

*Note:* This article is meant to be a fairly comprehensive guide that, if followed properly, will substantially increase your preparedness for the Advanced Placement Environmental Science examination. However, I cannot guarantee any single result on your classwork and AP exam. ## Understanding the Course & Exam
### Units of Study
APES has 9 units, all of which are fairly long. This class is chock-full of content, but, luckily, there's a lot of overlap and areas to compound your knowledge.

Unit 3, in my opinion, is the easiest unit, while unit 4 is the most difficult. The biogeochemical cycles were also difficult to remember. Unit 9 makes up the most of the exam, but is largely a combination of previous units and topics, so don't stress about it too much!

Here is a breakdown of key concepts in each topic/set of topics for you to learn:

**Unit 1:** The Living World - Ecosystems *(6-8% of the Exam)*
* 1.1 - Introduction to Ecosystems
  * Predator-prey relationships
  * Symbiosis - mutualism (+/+), commensalism (+/0), parasitism (+/-)
  * Competition, resource partitioning
  * Memorize these relationships and terms!
* 1.2-1.3 - Terrestrial and Aquatic Biomes
  * Taiga, rainforests, seasonal forests, desert, tundra, etc
  * Streams, rivers, oceans, coral reefs, etc
  * Memorize key characteristics of these biomes
  * Know how to read climatographs
* 1.4-1.7 - Biogeochemical Cycles
  * Carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, water cycles
  * Understand the key reservoirs and movements
  * All are powered by the sun except phosphorus, which doesn't have an atmospheric phase
* 1.8-1.11 - Energy & Matter Cycling
  * Net primary productivity = Gross primary productivity - Respiration
  * Trophic levels - producer, primary consumer, secondary consumer, etc
  * 10% of energy is transfered from one trophic level to the next
  * Arrows show the direction of movement in food webs/chains

**Unit 2:** The Living World - Biodiversity *(6-8% of the Exam)*
* 2.1 - Introduction to Biodiversity
  * 3 types - genetic, species, habitat
  * More biodiversity = better response to stressors
  * Species richness vs evenness
  * Specialist vs generalist species
* 2.2 - Ecosystem Services
  * Provisoning, regulating, cultural, supporting services
  * Regulating and supporting are the hardest to differentiate
* 2.3 - Island Biogeography
  * Most island species are specialists
  * Larger and closer islands have more species
  * Smaller and farther islands have less species
* 2.4-2.6 - Adaptations in Ecosystems
  * Tolerance - range of conditions an organizsm can survive in
  * Natural disruptions to ecosystems (ie. wildfires)
  * Adaptations can occur in both the short-term and long-term
* 2.7 - Ecological Succession
  * Primary succession - start from bare rock
  * Secondary succession - start from some soil
  * Pioneer, keystone, indicator species

**Unit 3:** Populations *(10-15% of the Exam)*
* 3.1-3.2 - Types of Species
  * Generalist vs specialist species
  * K-selected vs r-selected species
* 3.3-3.5 - General Population Dynamics
  * Type I, II, and III survivorship curves
  * Carrying capacity, overshoot, dieback
  * Population growth is limited by resources
* 3.6-3.9 - Human Populations
  * Age structure diagrams
  * Total fertility rate - 2.1 is replacement level
  * Birth rates, IMR, death rates
  * Rule of 70
  * Demographic transition model

**Unit 4:** Earth Systems and Resources *(10-15% of the Exam)*
* 4.1 - Plate Tectonics
  * Convergent, divergent, transform boundaries
  * San Andreas Fault
* 4.2-4.3 - Soil
  * Soil formation - weathering, deposition
  * OAEBCR soil horizons
  * Sand, silt, and clay
  * Permeability, porosity, fertility
  * Reading soil texture traingles
* 4.4-4.5 - Atmosphere & Wind
  * 78% Nitrogen, 21% Oxygen, 0.9% Argon
  * Troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, thermosphere, exosphere
  * Westerlies & eastern trade winds
  * Coriolis effect
* 4.6 - Watersheds
  * All of the land that drains into a body of water
  * Area, length, slope, soil, vegetation types
* 4.7-4.8 - Seasons & Climate
  * Angle of insolation, albedo
  * Solar radiation at the equator vs poles
  * Ocean's influence on weather and climate
  * Rain shadow effect
* 4.9 - El Nino and La Nina
  * Remember the difference!
  * Peruvian upwelling

**Unit 5:** Land and Water Use *(10-15% of the Exam)*
* 5.1 - The Tragedy of the Commons
   * Individuals use shared resources for their own good
   * Leads to depletion with resources
* 5.2 - Clearcutting
   * Economically advantageous
   * Leads to erosion, higher temperatures, climate change, etc
* 5.3-5.8 - Agriculture, Meat Production, & Fishing
   * The Green Revolution
   * Tilling, slash-and-burn, fertilizers
   * Drip, flood, furrow, spray irrigation
   * Pesticides, herbicides, fungicides, rodenticides, insecticides
   * CAFOs vs free-range
   * Overfishing
* 5.9 - Impacts of Mining
   * Surface, strip, subsurface mining
   * Tailings, slag, ore
* 5.10 - Impacts of Urbanization
   * Depletion of resources
   * Impervious surfaces
   * Urban sprawl
* 5.11 - Ecological Footprints
   * Resource usage and waste production relative to others
* 5.12-5.17 - Sustainable Practices
   * Sustainable yield
   * Reducing urban runoff
   * Integrated pest management - biological, physical, chemical controls
   * Crop rotation, green manure, rotational grazing
   * Aquaculture
   * Prescribed burns, reforestation, selective cutting

**Unit 6:** Energy Resources and Consumption *(10-15% of the Exam)*
* 6.1-6.4 - Energy Concepts
   * Renewable vs nonrenewable energy
   * Use in developed and developing countries
   * Wood, peat, methane, crude oil
   * Cogeneration
* 6.5 - Fossil Fuels
   * Combustion - fuel and oxygen -> CO2, water, energy
   * Combustion > heat > steam > turbine > generator
   * Fracking
* 6.6 - Nuclear Power
   * Uranium-235 & fission
   * Nonrenewable, but a cleaner energy source
   * Accidents, half-life, disposal
* 6.7 - Energy from Biomass
   * Developing countries
   * Produces CO2, PM, NOx, VOCs, etc
* 6.8-6.12 - Renewable Energy Sources
   * Active vs passive solar
   * Hydroelectric power
   * Geothermal energy
   * Hydrogen fuel cells
   * Wind turbines
* 6.13 - Energy Conservation
   * Adjusting thermostat, conserving water, etc
   * Hybrid vehicles, green building design, public transit

**Unit 7:** Atmospheric Pollution *(7-10% of the Exam)*
* 7.1 - Introduction to Air Pollution
   * Criteria pollutants - CO, Pb, NOx, O3, PM, SOx
   * Primary vs secondary pollutants
   * Effects of burning coal
* 7.2-7.3 - Atmospheric Air Pollution
   * Photochemical vs sulfurous smog
   * Thermal inversions
   * CO2 - natural and anthropogenic sources
* 7.5 - Indoor Air Pollution
   * CO, PM, VOCs, NOx, smoke, etc
   * Radon - produced by uranium decay in rocks
* 7.6 - Reduction of Air Pollutants
   * Regulatory, conservation, alternative fuels
   * Scrubbers, electrostatic precipitators
   * Vapor recovery nozzles, catalytic converters
* 7.7 - Acid Rain
   * Caused by NOx and SOx
   * Limestone neutralizes acid rain
* 7.8 - Noise Pollution
   * Transportation, construction, industrial activity
   * Negative effects on wildlife, causes stress

**Unit 8:** Aquatic and Terrestrial Pollution *(7-10% of the Exam)*
* 8.1-8.2 - Pollution and Human Disruption
   * Point vs nonpoint pollution
   * Oil spills, dead zones, sedimentation
   * Oxygen sag curves
* 8.3 - Endocrine Disruptors
   * BPAs, PFAS, BPAS, phalates, etc
* 8.4 - Human Impacts on Wetlands and Mangroves
   * Ecological services from wetlands
   * Commercial development, construction, pollutants
* 8.5-8.6 - Eutrophication and Thermal Pollution
   * Algal blooms, excess nutrients
   * Hypoxic, eutrophic, oligiotrophic waterways
   * Heat and biologic oxygen demand
   * Warm water doesn't contain as much oxygen
* 8.7-8.8 - POPs
   * Do not break down easily
   * Fat-soluble, accumulate in tissues
   * Bioaccumulation vs magnification
* 8.9-8.11 - Waste Disposal
   * Landfills, combustion, ocean dumping
   * Recycling, composting
   * Primary, secondary, tertiary treatment of sewage
* 8.12-8.13 - Doses
   * LD50
   * Dose-response curves
* 8.14-8.15 - Human Health & Pathogens
   * Dysentary, mesothelioma, respiratory issues
   * Malaria, plague, west nile virus, TB, zika, cholera

**Unit 9:** Global Change *(15-20% of the Exam)*
* 9.1-9.2: Ozone
   * Stratospheric ozone depletion
   * Montreal Protocol
* 9.3-9.5: Greenhouse Effect & Climate Change
   * CO2, methane, water vapor, NOx, CFCs
   * Natural vs anthropogenic climate change
* 9.6-9.7: Oceans
   * Ocean warming and coral bleaching
   * Ocean acidification, calcium carbonate shells
* 9.8-9.9: Types of Species
   * Invasive species - generalists, r-selected
   * Endangered species - pressures, competition, hunting
* 9.10: Human Impacts on Biodiversity
   * HIPPCO
   * Habitat fragmentation
   * Climate change

* Clean Water Act, SWDA

### The Exam
The exam lasts 2 hours and 40 minutes, and is fully on Bluebook.

**Section 1:** 80 Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)
*1 hour, 30 minutes | 60% of exam score*
* Individual & Set-based questions
* 3-4 sets with data tables, charts or graphs
* 3-4 sets with models, represetnations, or maps
* 2 sets with text-based sources
* Covers all 9 units
* Each question is worth 1 point

**Section 2:** 3 Free Response Questions (FRQs)
*1 hour, 10 mintes | 40% of exam score*
* 3 questions, each with ~5-8 sub-parts
* Question 1 - design an investigation
* Question 2 - analyze an environmental problem, with a model
* Question 3 - analyze an environmental problem, with calculations
* Covers all 9 units
* Each FRQ is scored out of 10 points

> Make sure to choose an answer or write something for all parts of the exam - you can't lose points, only gain them!

### The Curve
APES is generally considered an 'easier' AP exam, meaning that you have to score higher to get a 5. Luckily, there's still room for error. You only need a 96/130, or roughly 74%, to get a 5.

| Raw Score | AP Score | What does this mean?                                            | % Received Score (2026) |
| --------- | -------- | ------------------------------------------                      | -------------------- |
| 74-100%   | 5        | ✅ Excellent! Most colleges will accept this.                      | 13% |
| 59-73%    | 4        | 🎉 Great job! Some colleges will accept this.                      | 29% |
| 52-58%    | 3        | 👍 Nice! A few colleges will accept this.                          | 27% |
| 38-51%    | 2        | 😐 You'll have a better foundation for college.         | 15% |
| 0-37%     | 1        | 🧠 You challenged yourself with college level coursework. | 16% |

> Fun fact: The average score on the 2026 AP Environmental Science exam was not a 3. Mathematically, it was a 3.08.

I would recommend shooting for a 80-85% to be solidly in the 5 range. Try to get at least a 65/80 on the MCQs, and 8/10 on the FRQs. You can adjust these numbers as needed if you're better at multiple choice questions or writing.
